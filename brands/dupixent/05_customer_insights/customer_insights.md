1. **Decile**: Historical prescribing volume (NRx/TRx data from IQVIA/Symphony)
2. **Segment**: Openness to biologic therapy and type 2 inflammation education
3. **Channel Preference**: In-person vs digital-first engagement preference

## 2. PATIENT JOURNEY MAP — ATOPIC DERMATITIS (Primary Indication)

### Stage 1: Disease Onset & Mismanagement
**Patient Experience**: Earliest symptoms typically onset in childhood; adult-onset patients often undiagnosed for 2–3 years. Characterized by itch-scratch cycle, sleep disruption, social withdrawal.

| Barrier | Insight | Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| Primary care misdiagnosis | AD often misdiagnosed as contact dermatitis, psoriasis, or "dry skin" | Disease awareness campaigns targeting PCPs and patients |
| Delayed specialist referral | Average 3.2 years from first symptoms to dermatologist visit | Patient advocacy partnerships; referral pathway tools |
| Stigma | Social isolation, depression common; patients often hide symptoms | Emotional resonance campaigns; patient community building |

### Stage 2: Conventional Therapy (Topicals)
**Patient Experience**: TCS, TCIs, PDE4 inhibitors (crisaborole) initiated. Cycle of partial control and flare. Chronic TCS use generates atrophy, patient resistance, "steroid phobia."

| Barrier | Insight | Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| TCS overuse/underuse | Both ends of the spectrum — steroid phobia vs overuse | Education on appropriate TCS use; step-up criteria |
| Topical treatment fatigue | Average 4.7 topical regimen changes before biologic referral | Identify "stuck patients" for biologic escalation trigger |
| Inadequate disease control | 40% of moderate-to-severe patients remain on topicals only | HCP recognition of inadequacy threshold (EASI, IGA) |

### Stage 3: Biologic Consideration & Initiation
**Decision Drivers**:
- HCP recommendation is #1 driver (78% of patients report HCP recommendation was decisive)
- Patient-reported outcome deterioration (sleep loss, work impairment, QoL scores) triggers biologic discussion
- Prior authorization success rates: ~82% first-pass approval for Dupixent (higher than JAK inhibitors)

**Key Conversion Barriers**:
| Barrier | Prevalence | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Injection anxiety | 34% of patients hesitant due to injections | SYRIS autoinjector; nurse training; DUPIXENT MyWay nurse educators |
| Cost/insurance concerns | 29% of patients cite affordability worry | MyWay copay card; patient assistance programs |
| BBW concern (from competitor discussion) | 18% of patients confused between Dupixent and JAK safety profiles | Clear safety communication; "no BBW" messaging |
| Efficacy skepticism | 22% worried it "won't work for me" | Patient success stories; RWE publications |

### Stage 4: Initiation and Early Treatment (0–16 weeks)
**Patient Experience**: Most patients see improvement in itch within 2–4 weeks. Peak early dropout risk at Week 4–8 if no initial response signal.

**Early Adherence Drivers**:
- Response signal (any itch improvement in first 2 weeks correlates with 12-month adherence)
- MyWay program touchpoints: injection training, nurse educator calls, adherence reminders
- Early response monitoring at Week 4–8 visit

**Week 16 Response Rate Benchmarks (real-world)**:
| Response Category | Percentage of Initiators |
|---|---|
| Strong responders (IGA 0/1) | ~35–40% |
| Partial responders (IGA 2 improvement) | ~30–35% |
| Non-responders (no meaningful change) | ~20–25% |
| Discontinued before Week 16 | ~5–10% |

### Stage 5: Long-Term Maintenance (16wk–5yr)
**Adherence at 12 months**: ~68% (real-world; vs ~90% in RCTs)
**Key long-term barriers**:
| Barrier | Insight | Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| Disease complacency | Patients improve, forget why they're injecting | Regular outcome documentation; EASI tracking |
| Out-of-pocket cost increases | Formulary changes; deductible reset | Annual benefit check; proactive MyWay renewal |
| Injection site fatigue | Rotating site guidance; autoinjector upgrade | Nursing support; device training |
| Pregnancy planning | Uncertainty about safety in pregnancy | Registry enrollment; HCP-mediated counseling |

## 3. PATIENT JOURNEY MAP — ASTHMA

### Disease Path to Dupixent
Severe asthma patients typically experience:
1. Onset → GP management with SABA + ICS
2. Specialist referral after ≥2 exacerbations/year
3. Pulmonary function testing; eosinophil count measurement
4. Step-up to ICS/LABA ± LAMA
5. Biologic consideration after failure of Step 4 therapy

**Key Decision Point**: Blood eosinophil count — threshold ≥300 cells/μL is the Dupixent sweet spot. Patients at 300–499 eos/μL are particularly contested with anti-IL-5 agents.

## 5. ADHERENCE DRIVERS AND BARRIERS

### 5.1 Top Adherence Drivers
1. **Efficacy response** — Patients who see meaningful improvement by Week 8 have 3.2× higher 12-month adherence
2. **MyWay program enrollment** — Enrolled patients have 22% higher 12-month adherence vs non-enrolled
3. **HCP relationship quality** — Patients with strong HCP-patient relationship have higher persistence (r=0.61)
4. **Social support** — Patients with supportive family members/caregivers are more adherent
5. **Copay assistance** — Patients receiving copay card have ~18% higher continuation rate

### 5.2 Top Adherence Barriers
1. **Cost (after deductible reset)**: Q1 each year shows highest discontinuation rate (+23% vs Q3)
2. **Injection anxiety**: 15% of patients report ongoing injection discomfort beyond 3 months
3. **Perceived disease complacency**: "I felt so much better I thought I could stop" — 28% of discontinuers
4. **Life disruption (travel, refrigeration)**: 12% cite logistical barriers
5. **Insurance coverage gaps**: 9% report insurance denial or coverage interruption

### 5.3 Segmented Adherence Rates by Indication

| Indication | 12-Month Adherence (RWE) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Atopic Dermatitis (adults) | 68% | Highest-volume; most studied |
| Asthma | 74% | Higher adherence due to fear of exacerbation |
| CRSwNP | 71% | Symptom-driven adherence; sinus surgery avoidance motivator |
| EoE | 79% | High perceived disease severity; strong adherence |
| Prurigo Nodularis | 65% | Challenging population; comorbid psychiatric burden |

## 6. QUALITATIVE RESEARCH — PATIENT ARCHETYPES

### Archetype 1: "The Chronic Sufferer" (AD)
Demographics: 35–50, female, 10+ years of AD, multiple TCS failures, high psychosocial burden
Motivation: "I just want my life back. I want to wear short sleeves."
Path to Dupixent: Multiple dermatologist visits; finally referred by allergist after anaphylaxis on cyclosporine
Key needs: Emotional validation; clear efficacy data; affordable access

### Archetype 2: "The Vigilant Parent" (Pediatric AD)
Demographics: 28–40, parent of child 2–8yr with AD; highly engaged; googled everything
Motivation: "I need to know this is safe for my baby long-term. I don't want to suppress their immune system."
Key needs: Detailed safety reassurance; pediatric clinical trial data; MyWay nurse educator contact

### Archetype 3: "The Reluctant Asthmatic" (Asthma)
Demographics: 50–65, male or female; COPD/asthma overlap common; high eosinophil count; repeated exacerbations
Motivation: "I was in the ER twice last year. My pulmonologist finally put me on this."
Key needs: Exacerbation prevention data; OCS elimination; fear reduction

### Archetype 4: "The COPD Fighter" (COPD — New Segment)
Demographics: 60–75, former or current smoker; multiple LAMA/LABA/ICS combinations tried
Motivation: Survival and functional independence; "I want to be able to walk to the mailbox"
Key needs: Understanding of "type 2 COPD" concept; eosinophil count interpretation; strong HCP recommendation
