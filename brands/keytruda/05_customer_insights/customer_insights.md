## 1. HCP Segmentation Framework

### Tier Definition Criteria
| Tier | Annual KEYTRUDA Prescriptions (TRx) | Account Type | Engagement Model |
|------|-------------------------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Tier 1 — Platinum** | ≥200 TRx/year | Academic/NCI Cancer Center | Senior MSL, Senior Rep, Account Director; Advisory Board eligible |
| **Tier 2 — Gold** | 80–199 TRx/year | Community Cancer Centers, Large Group Practice | Regular Rep visits + MSL; speaker bureau eligible |
| **Tier 3 — Silver** | 30–79 TRx/year | Mid-size community oncology practice | Quarterly rep visits; digital engagement |
| **Tier 4 — Bronze** | <30 TRx/year | Small community, rural oncology | Digital/virtual engagement; remote MSL support |

### Tier 1 Profile: Academic Oncologist (KOL)
- **Profile**: Research-active, publishes trials data, shapes NCCN guidelines, mentors fellows
- **Key Priorities**: Novel combinations, biomarker precision, clinical trial enrollment, safety differentiation
- **Information Needs**: Pipeline data, mechanism of action nuances, irAE algorithm refinement, Phase 1/2 early readouts
- **Engagement Preference**: Medical Science Liaison (MSL) relationship; data presentations; advisory board invitations
- **Pain Points**: High irAE burden; lack of predictive biomarkers for irAE; combination sequencing complexity
- **Brand Perception**: Generally positive; views KEYTRUDA as clinical gold standard; wants differentiation beyond "me-too" indications

### Tier 2 Profile: Community Oncologist (High Volume)
- **Profile**: Sees 40–80 cancer patients/week; mix of tumor types; limited research involvement
- **Key Priorities**: Practical dosing, infusion logistics, patient support, formulary access, AE management
- **Information Needs**: Dosing guides, irAE management algorithms, patient support programs, prior auth tools
- **Engagement Preference**: Regular rep visits (every 4–6 weeks); webinar CMEs; lunch-and-learn programs
- **Pain Points**: Infusion chair time constraints (QLEX SC directly addresses), PA denial rates, patient affordability
- **Brand Perception**: High confidence; KEYTRUDA is typically first-choice; SC formulation highly anticipated

### Tier 3 Profile: Small Community/Rural Oncologist
- **Profile**: Solo or 2–3 physician practice; refers complex cases to academic center; general oncology
- **Key Priorities**: Simplicity, guideline alignment, reimbursement support, patient navigation
- **Information Needs**: Clear dosing schedules; eligibility criteria; patient assistance program details
- **Engagement Preference**: Digital touchpoints; telemedicine CME; KEYTRUDA.com resources
- **Pain Points**: Lack of in-house infusion capability (QLEX SC is significant opportunity); biosimilar confusion; time burden for PA

## 3. Patient Journey — NSCLC (PD-L1 High, 1L Treatment)

### Stage 1: Diagnosis & Staging
- **Patient Experience**: Shock, anxiety; high information need; may search online before HCP counseling
- **Barriers**: Delayed biopsy / PD-L1 testing turnaround (median 7–12 days); inadequate tissue for molecular testing
- **Opportunities**: Patient education materials at diagnosis; KEYTRUDA testing support line; navigator programs
- **HCP Touchpoint**: Pulmonologist/thoracic surgeon referring to medical oncologist; pathologist issuing biomarker report

### Stage 2: Treatment Decision
- **Patient Experience**: Information overload; desire for "the best" treatment; fear of side effects
- **Barriers**: Miscommunication about PD-L1 results; lack of second opinion access in rural settings
- **Opportunities**: KEYTRUDA patient brochures in oncologist office; shared decision-making tools; QLEX SC preference discussion
- **HCP Touchpoint**: First medical oncology visit; molecular tumor board; nurse navigator introduction

### Stage 3: Treatment Initiation
- **Patient Experience**: Anxiety about first infusion; concerns about immune side effects; scheduling logistics
- **Barriers**: Insurance pre-authorization delays (avg 6.2 days); financial toxicity; childcare/transportation
- **Opportunities**: KEYTRUDA Patient Access Program; co-pay support; infusion scheduling coordination
- **HCP Touchpoint**: Infusion nurse; pharmacist; case manager; Merck access teams

### Stage 4: Ongoing Treatment / Monitoring
- **Patient Experience**: Fatigue commonly reported; adaptation to treatment rhythm; monitoring for irAEs
- **Barriers**: Under-reporting of symptoms; delayed identification of irAEs; discontinuation due to toxicity (7–15%)
- **Opportunities**: Patient monitoring apps; symptom diaries; QLEX SC reduces infusion burden (6–10 clinic trips/year vs. prior Q3W/Q6W); telehealth follow-up
- **HCP Touchpoint**: Repeat labs (LFTs, thyroid, cortisol), imaging Q6–9 weeks; regular symptom review

### Stage 5: Response Assessment & Continuation
- **Patient Experience**: Anxiety around scan results; "scanxiety"; hope for continued response
- **Barriers**: Pseudo-progression misidentification; imaging frequency burden
- **Opportunities**: Response visualization tools; patient support groups; survivorship planning resources
- **HCP Touchpoint**: Medical oncologist; radiologist; genetic counselor (family screening)

### Stage 6: Discontinuation / Survivorship / Progression
- **Patient Experience**: Varies widely — joy at treatment completion vs. fear of recurrence; grief at progression
- **Barriers**: Unclear stopping rules in community oncology; lack of survivorship care plan
- **Opportunities**: Long-term survivor programs; post-pembrolizumab monitoring guidance; re-treatment protocols (retreatment with pembrolizumab supported by data in some settings)

## 5. Payer & Patient Barriers

### Payer Barriers
| Barrier | Payer Type | Impact | Mitigation |
|---------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Step therapy (chemotherapy first in low PD-L1) | Commercial / Medicaid | HIGH | NCCN guideline alignment; biomarker exemption advocacy |
| Prior authorization complexity (4–8 items required) | All payers | HIGH | PAH support services; specialty pharmacy facilitation |
| Site-of-care restrictions (hospital vs. community) | Commercial | MEDIUM | SC formulation enables lower-cost admin settings |
| High patient OOP (deductibles, copays) | Commercial ACA plans | HIGH | KEYTRUDA Cost Support Program (co-pay cards) |
| Biosimilar mandate (forthcoming 2028+) | PBM/MCO | HIGH | QLEX SC differentiation; HEOR dossiers |
| International reimbursement gaps (HTA rejection) | UK NICE, France HAS | MEDIUM | Supplemental HEOR submissions; real-world data |

### Patient Barriers
- Annual drug cost: ~$252,000 WAC for IV; co-pay programs reduce patient out-of-pocket to $0–$100/month for eligible commercially insured
- Medicaid patients: Many states cover KEYTRUDA; federal rebate program applies
- Medicare Part B: Administered in physician office; subject to 20% coinsurance; Medicare Savings Programs available
- Uninsured/underinsured: Merck Access Program provides free drug to eligible patients (income threshold varies by indication)
- Transportation barrier: 38% of rural patients report transportation as barrier to infusion center access — QLEX SC with potential home self-injection to address
