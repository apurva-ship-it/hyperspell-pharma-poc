# Entresto (sacubitril/valsartan) — Brand Intelligence

**Generic Name:** Sacubitril / valsartan  
**Brand:** Entresto  
**Company:** Novartis  
**Drug Class:** Angiotensin Receptor-Neprilysin Inhibitor (ARNI)  
**Mechanism of Action:** Dual mechanism — sacubitril (a prodrug converted to LBQ657) inhibits **neprilysin**, preventing degradation of natriuretic peptides (BNP, ANP, CNP), bradykinin, and other vasoactive peptides → vasodilation, natriuresis, reduced fibrosis; valsartan blocks **AT1 receptors** → vasodilation, reduced aldosterone. Combined effect: reduced preload/afterload, reverse cardiac remodeling.  
**FDA Approval:** July 2015 (HFrEF); February 2021 (HFpEF — exploratory; all EF ranges with elevated NT-proBNP)  
**Formulation:** 24/26 mg, 49/51 mg, 97/103 mg oral tablets (sacubitril/valsartan); BID dosing  

---

## Approved Indications (US)

1. **Heart Failure (HFrEF):** Reduce risk of cardiovascular death and hospitalization for HF in adults with chronic HF with reduced ejection fraction (LVEF ≤40%); generally used as replacement for ACE inhibitor or ARB
2. **Heart Failure (broader label, 2021):** Adults with chronic symptomatic HF with elevated NT-proBNP — encompasses HFpEF and HFmrEF with biomarker confirmation

### Pediatric Indication (2019)
- Heart failure in children ≥1 year with systemic left ventricular systolic dysfunction

## Clinical Efficacy

### PARADIGM-HF (HFrEF — landmark pivotal trial)
- **Design:** Sacubitril/valsartan vs enalapril (ACE inhibitor), 8,442 patients with HFrEF (LVEF ≤40%), NYHA II–IV; median follow-up 27 months
- **Primary composite endpoint:** CV death or first HF hospitalization
- **Results:**
  - **Primary endpoint:** 21.8% vs 26.5% (HR **0.80**, 95% CI 0.73–0.87, p<0.0001)
  - **CV death:** 13.3% vs 16.5% (HR **0.80**, p<0.0001) — **20% relative risk reduction**
  - **HF hospitalization:** 12.8% vs 15.6% (HR **0.79**, p<0.0001)
  - **All-cause mortality:** 17.0% vs 19.8% (HR **0.84**, p=0.0002)
  - **NYHA class improvement:** More patients improved, fewer worsened vs enalapril
- **Trial stopped early** at pre-specified interim analysis — overwhelming efficacy
- **NNT (CV death or HFH):** 21 patients treated for 27 months to prevent 1 event

### PARADIGM-HF Subgroup Analyses
- Consistent benefit across EF strata (≤35%, 35–40%), NYHA class, age, sex
- **De novo HF patients:** HR 0.71 (greater benefit in early HF)
- **Diabetic patients:** Consistent benefit + lower new-onset T2D vs enalapril

### PARAGON-HF (HFpEF — EF >45%)
- **Design:** Sacubitril/valsartan vs valsartan in HFpEF (LVEF ≥45%)
- **Primary endpoint:** CV death or HF hospitalization — **not statistically significant** (HR 0.87, p=0.06)
- **Key subgroup:** Women and lower EF (45–57%) showed signal for benefit
- **FDA label extension (2021):** Based on PARAGON-HF + pooled data; expanded to all EF with elevated NT-proBNP (biomarker-selected)

### PIONEER-HF (Acute HF hospitalization — inpatient initiation)
- **Design:** In-hospital initiation of sacubitril/valsartan vs enalapril post-acute HF stabilization
- **NT-proBNP reduction at 8 weeks:** −47% vs −25% (sacubitril/valsartan; p<0.001)
- **Exploratory composite (death/rehospitalization/HF event):** HR 0.65 (favors ARNI)
- **Safety:** No difference in worsening renal function, hyperkalemia, or hypotension vs enalapril
- **Implication:** Supports in-hospital initiation before discharge rather than waiting

### EVALUATE-HF (Echocardiographic outcomes)
- Sacubitril/valsartan vs enalapril in HFrEF
- **Aortic stiffness (LAVI):** Trend toward improvement; cardiac remodeling markers favorable
- NT-proBNP significantly reduced

### Real-World Evidence
- **CHAMP-HF registry:** Sacubitril/valsartan associated with lower 30-day and 1-year mortality vs ACE/ARB in propensity-matched analysis
- **BIOSTAT-CHF:** Biomarker-guided initiation improves outcomes

## Safety Profile

### Common Adverse Events
| AE | Frequency (vs enalapril) |
|---|---|
| Hypotension | 14.0% vs 9.2% |
| Hyperkalemia | 11.6% vs 14.0% (lower than enalapril) |
| Renal impairment | 10.2% vs 11.5% (similar) |
| Cough | 11.3% vs 14.3% (lower — key advantage vs ACE inhibitor) |
| Dizziness | 18.0% vs 17.4% |
| Angioedema | 0.45% vs 0.24% (higher; key warning) |

### Serious Adverse Events
| AE | Notes |
|---|---|
| Angioedema | Higher than ACE inhibitors; history of ACE inhibitor-related angioedema contraindicated |
| Symptomatic hypotension | More common; initiate at low dose (24/26 mg BID); titrate over 2–4 weeks |
| Hyperkalemia | Monitor K+ especially with CKD, diabetes, or concurrent K-sparing agents |
| Renal impairment | Monitor serum creatinine and eGFR at initiation and titration |

### Black Box Warning
- **Embryo-fetal toxicity (via valsartan):** Drugs acting on the renin-angiotensin system can cause fetal injury and death; discontinue when pregnancy detected

### Key Contraindications
- **Concomitant ACE inhibitor use:** Must wait ≥36 hours after last ACE inhibitor dose before starting (risk of angioedema)
- **Prior ACE inhibitor-related angioedema:** Contraindicated
- **Concomitant aliskiren** in patients with diabetes
- **Severe hepatic impairment** (Child-Pugh C)

## Competitive Landscape

### HFrEF Treatment Pillars (Four Pillars of GDMT)
Modern HFrEF management uses four evidence-based drug classes together:

| Pillar | Class | Agents |
|---|---|---|
| **ARNI/RAAS** | ARNI (preferred) or ACE-I/ARB | **Entresto**, Lisinopril, Losartan |
| **Beta-blocker** | Selective BB | Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate, Bisoprolol |
| **MRA** | Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist | Spironolactone, Eplerenone |
| **SGLT2i** | SGLT2 inhibitor | Jardiance (empagliflozin), Farxiga (dapagliflozin) |

### Entresto vs ACE Inhibitors (direct class competition)
| Parameter | Entresto | ACE Inhibitor (enalapril) |
|---|---|---|
| CV death reduction | −20% (PARADIGM-HF) | Historical 15–20% |
| Cough | 11.3% | 14.3% |
| Angioedema | Higher (0.45%) | Lower (0.24%) |
| Cost | High (branded) | Very low (generic) |
| Mechanism | Dual (ARNI) | Single (RAAS only) |

**Guideline position:** ACC/AHA/HFSA 2022 guidelines: ARNI (Entresto) is **Class I, LOE B-R** recommendation — preferred over ACE inhibitor or ARB in HFrEF.

### SGLT2i Co-prescribing
- DAPA-HF (dapagliflozin) and EMPEROR-Reduced (empagliflozin) showed additive benefit on top of ARNI
- Current standard: Combine Entresto + SGLT2 inhibitor + beta-blocker + MRA
- Market dynamic: SGLT2 inhibitors do NOT compete with Entresto; they are complementary and add to overall prescription burden (multiple specialist drugs)

### Generic Pressure
- Sacubitril/valsartan US patent expected 2025–2026; generic applications filed
- Novartis pursuing authorized generic strategy
- International markets: Some generics approved in select EU countries

## Key HCP Messages

1. **The only heart failure drug to simultaneously reduce CV death, HF hospitalization, and all-cause mortality vs ACE inhibitor:** In PARADIGM-HF, sacubitril/valsartan achieved a 20% reduction in CV death, 21% reduction in HF hospitalization, and 16% reduction in all-cause mortality — across all major endpoints simultaneously.

2. **Guideline-preferred over ACE inhibitors in HFrEF (Class I):** The 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA Heart Failure Guidelines recommend ARNI as preferred over ACE inhibitor or ARB (Class I, LOE B-R) for all HFrEF patients able to tolerate it — based on PARADIGM-HF superiority evidence.

3. **Better tolerated than ACE inhibitors in key AEs:** Entresto produces significantly less cough (11.3% vs 14.3%) and lower hyperkalemia rates compared to enalapril — two of the most common reasons for ACE inhibitor discontinuation in practice.

4. **In-hospital initiation is safe and improves biomarkers:** PIONEER-HF demonstrated that initiating sacubitril/valsartan in-hospital during an acute HF admission achieves 47% NT-proBNP reduction at 8 weeks vs 25% with enalapril, with no safety penalty — enabling earlier treatment intensification.

5. **Expanded benefit across EF spectrum:** The 2021 FDA label expansion supports use in HFpEF/HFmrEF patients with elevated NT-proBNP, extending eligibility to a broader population beyond traditional HFrEF.

## Patient Profile

### Ideal HFrEF Patient
- LVEF ≤40%, symptomatic (NYHA II–IV), stable outpatient
- Currently on ACE inhibitor or ARB (switch to Entresto; 36-hour washout from ACE-I)
- SBP ≥100 mmHg at initiation
- eGFR ≥30 mL/min/1.73m² (lower eGFR: proceed with caution, dose-reduce)
- K+ <5.0 mEq/L at initiation

### Ideal HFpEF Patient
- LVEF ≥40%, symptomatic, elevated NT-proBNP
- Women and patients with EF 45–57% appear to derive greatest benefit (PARAGON-HF)
- No current ACE inhibitor (angioedema risk if recent ACE-I)

### Initiation Dose
- ACE-I/ARB-naïve or low-dose: Start 24/26 mg BID → uptitrate every 2–4 weeks
- Currently on moderate/high-dose ACE-I/ARB: Start 49/51 mg BID
- Target dose: 97/103 mg BID (achieved in PARADIGM-HF median)

### Key Exclusions
- SBP <100 mmHg (hypotension risk)
- Severe renal impairment (eGFR <30) — use with extreme caution
- Prior angioedema with ACE inhibitor/ARB
- Pregnancy

## Market Position

### Sales Performance
- **2023 Global Revenue:** ~$3.8 billion USD
- **2022 Revenue:** ~$3.2 billion USD (+19% growth)
- **US Revenue (2023):** ~$2.3 billion
- **Growth drivers:** HFpEF label expansion; increased guideline adherence; SGLT2 co-prescribing driving cardiologist visits
- **Penetration gap:** Despite Class I guidelines, only ~20–25% of eligible HFrEF patients in the US receive ARNI vs ACE/ARB

### Access & Payer Dynamics
- **Commercial:** Generally covered Tier 2–3; prior auth required in some plans (proof of ACE-I/ARB failure or intolerance)
- **Medicare:** Part D coverage; IRA price negotiation candidate for future cycles
- **Specialty pharmacy:** Required at some health systems; Novartis Entresto co-pay card program
- **Cost barrier:** ~$600–700/month list vs <$10/month for generic lisinopril — physician and patient hesitancy

### Key Threats
- Generic entry 2025–2026 (sacubitril/valsartan patents)
- Under-prescribing due to cost and complexity in primary care setting (cardiology-specialist skew)
- SGLT2 inhibitors gaining share in HFpEF where Entresto evidence is limited
