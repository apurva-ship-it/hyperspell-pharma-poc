# Patient Journey Map — EGFR+ NSCLC
**Brand:** Nexorel | **Document Type:** Patient Journey & Insight Map
**Research Basis:** 42 in-depth patient interviews + 6 oncologist focus groups (Aether Oncology Market Research, Q3 2025)

---

## Patient Archetype: "Margaret, 61, Former Teacher"

*Composite of 14 patient interviews. Represents the median EGFR+ NSCLC patient in the US.*

- Age: 61 | Non-smoker | Active lifestyle pre-diagnosis
- Diagnosis: Stage IV NSCLC with EGFR exon 19 deletion + 2 small brain metastases
- Treatment: Nexorel 100mg QD (Month 4 of treatment at interview)
- Support: Husband, adult daughter, primary oncologist at regional cancer center

---

## Stage 1: Pre-Diagnosis — "Something is Wrong"

**Duration:** Typically 3–6 months before formal diagnosis

**Patient Experience:**
- Persistent cough attributed to allergies or acid reflux
- Fatigue dismissed as "getting older"
- Shortness of breath noticed during activities previously manageable
- First HCP visit often to primary care or pulmonologist, not oncologist

**Key Emotions:** Dismissal, normalization of symptoms, mild anxiety

**Patient Quote:**
> "I kept thinking it was just stress. I was sleeping more, not running as much. My PCP said my chest X-ray looked fine. It wasn't until I started having headaches that we did a CT."

**Brand Opportunity:** Not applicable at this stage. However, awareness campaigns about persistent respiratory symptoms and lung cancer screening (LDCT) could shorten time to diagnosis.

**HCP Behavior:** Primary care and pulmonologists are gatekeepers — Nexorel's medical education for pulmonologists on EGFR testing protocols is relevant here.

---

## Stage 2: Diagnosis & Biomarker Testing — "The World Stops"

**Duration:** 2–4 weeks from imaging to confirmed pathology + biomarker result

**Patient Experience:**
- CT/PET scan reveals lung mass — immediate fear of cancer
- Biopsy arranged (CT-guided or bronchoscopy)
- Waiting for pathology: described universally as "the worst 2 weeks"
- Oncologist referral and first oncology visit
- Learning about EGFR testing — most patients had never heard of biomarker testing

**Critical Pain Points:**
- Confusion about "what EGFR means" and why it matters
- Anxiety during wait for biomarker results (adds 7–14 days to the anxious waiting period)
- Geographic disparity: patients at academic centers get NGS reflexively; community patients often need advocacy from oncologist

**Patient Quote:**
> "My oncologist said we needed to wait for a special test. I didn't understand why we weren't just starting treatment. Every day felt like a day the cancer was growing."

**Brand Opportunity:** Nexorel's Biomarker Testing Support program — provides patient-facing educational materials ("Why We Test Before We Treat") and co-funds NGS testing education at community oncology practices.

**Key Metric:** Median time from biopsy to EGFR result receipt by oncologist: 12.3 days (community) vs. 7.1 days (academic). Nexorel targets <7 days across all settings through Foundation Medicine and Guardant partnerships.

## Stage 3: Treatment Decision — "The Handoff"

**Duration:** 1–3 oncology visits over 1–2 weeks

**Patient Experience:**
- Oncologist presents treatment options (usually Tagrisso or Nexorel in EGFR+ setting)
- Patients report information overload — survival statistics, side effects, pill vs. IV
- High trust in oncologist recommendation — most patients do not self-research at this stage
- Brain metastases add layer of complexity: radiation vs. systemic therapy discussion
- Patient support navigator contact often happens here

**What Drives the Decision (Oncologist-reported):**
1. Efficacy data they trust (35%)
2. Their own clinical experience (28%)
3. Payer/formulary access (21%)
4. Side effect profile (11%)
5. Patient preference (5%)

**Patient Quote:**
> "He said there was a new drug that was especially good if the cancer had gone to the brain. Mine had. He said the data was very clear. I trusted him completely."

**Brand Opportunity:** The oncologist's language at this visit often becomes the patient's language for years. "Especially good for brain" is exactly the message Nexorel trains physicians to communicate. Peer-to-peer programs and speaker bureaus are the highest-leverage touchpoints here.

**Navigator Program Trigger:** Nexorel Navigator call initiated same day as prescription — 89% contact rate within 24 hours in pilot.

## Stage 4: Treatment Initiation — "Learning to Live With It"

**Duration:** First 4–12 weeks on therapy

**Patient Experience:**
- Starting medication — "this little pill is fighting cancer" is a common framing
- Managing side effects: diarrhea most common early complaint (Nexorel: 38%), rash on hands/face
- Establishing new routines around medication timing
- First follow-up CT at 6–8 weeks: high anxiety, then relief if responding
- Brain MRI at 3 months: "the scan I was most scared of"

**Key Moments of Truth:**
- **Day 1:** Does the patient understand how to take it correctly? Nurse educator call critical.
- **Week 2:** Early diarrhea — if not managed, adherence drops. Nexorel Navigator proactive call at Day 14.
- **Week 6–8:** First response scan — enormous emotional weight. Oncologist communication critical.

**Patient Quote:**
> "The first scan came back and the tumor was smaller. I cried for an hour. Then I asked — what about the brain? He said the brain scans looked stable. That was the moment I felt like I might actually get through this."

**Adherence Data (NEXUS-PRO sub-study):**
- Medication Possession Ratio at 6 months: 91% (Nexorel) vs. 84% (osimertinib historical)
- Missed doses: average 1.8/month (Nexorel) vs. 2.9/month (osimertinib historical)

**Brand Opportunity:** Nexorel's adherence advantage is real but fragile in first 90 days. Investment in nurse educator calls at Day 14 and Day 45 significantly improves 6-month MPR.

## Stage 5: Long-Term Therapy — "The New Normal"

**Duration:** 12+ months for responding patients

**Patient Experience:**
- Treatment becomes routine; less anxiety around scans (desensitization)
- Return to some normal activities — patients on Nexorel report higher activity levels than historical TKI experience (QoL sub-study)
- Ongoing side effect management: paronychia becomes the primary chronic complaint
- Concerns shift to: "How long will this work?" and "What happens when it stops?"
- Community and support groups become important for emotional coping

**Patient Quote:**
> "I've been on this pill for 18 months. I go to yoga again. People at work don't know I have cancer. But in the back of my mind I'm always waiting for the scan that says it stopped working."

**Key Fear: Resistance**
- Most EGFR+ patients eventually develop resistance (MET amplification, EGFR C797S mutation)
- Patients don't understand resistance mechanistically — they experience it as "the drug failing me"
- Psychological preparation for resistance discussion is an unmet need — only 31% of patients felt prepared when their disease progressed

**Brand Opportunity:** Nexorel's Resistance Readiness Program (in development) — patient and caregiver education on what resistance means, what re-biopsy involves, and what next options look like. Positions Aether as a long-term partner beyond Nexorel.

## Caregiver Journey Summary

**Key Insight:** Caregivers (typically spouses) carry significant anxiety burden that exceeds patient-reported anxiety in structured assessments. Yet caregiver support is a significant gap.

**Caregiver Needs:**
- Practical: medication management support, appointment scheduling help
- Emotional: peer connection with other caregivers
- Informational: "What do I do when the drug stops working?"

**Navigator Enhancement:** Nexorel Navigator added caregiver-specific touchpoints in January 2026. Early data shows 18% improvement in caregiver-reported support satisfaction.
