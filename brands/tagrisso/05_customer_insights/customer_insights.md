## 2. PATIENT JOURNEY MAP — FIRST-LINE METASTATIC NSCLC

### Stage 1: Symptom Presentation and Diagnosis
**Patient demographic**: Median age ~60–65; high proportion (30–40%) never-smokers; female predominance in never-smoker EGFR-mutant; East Asian ancestry overrepresented

**Common presenting symptoms**: Dyspnea, cough, chest pain; fatigue; weight loss; hemoptysis
**Diagnostic workup**: CT → PET → bronchoscopy/EBUS/CTGB biopsy → histopathology → molecular testing

| Barrier | Insight | Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| Delayed molecular testing | EGFR results take 10–14 days (tissue NGS); delays 1st-line start | Liquid biopsy (ctDNA) can turn around in 3–5 days; bridge to tissue results |
| Testing failures | ~15% of tissue biopsies yield insufficient material for NGS | Pre-test liquid biopsy strategy; reflexive liquid biopsy protocol |
| Test ordering gaps | ~22% of advanced NSCLC patients globally do not receive EGFR testing | Physician education; reflex testing protocols embedded in pathology |

### Stage 2: Treatment Decision and Initiation
**Patient experience**: Diagnosis often received as a shock; patients seek to understand "why me" (especially never-smokers); want to understand treatment options and prognosis

**Tagrisso Selection Criteria (HCP decision drivers)**:
1. EGFR Ex19del or L858R confirmed on FDA-approved CDx test
2. NCCN Category 1 recommendation (preferred agent)
3. Patient preference: oral daily tablet vs IV infusion (MARIPOSA combo)
4. CNS disease status: brain mets present → Tagrisso CNS protection is critical driver
5. Performance status: PS 0–2 → Tagrisso monotherapy appropriate
6. Comorbidities: ILD risk factors, cardiac history (QTc assessment)

**Patient Education at Initiation**:
- "Why Tagrisso" explanation: EGFR as driver; targeted vs chemotherapy
- Mechanism: "blocking the signal that tells your tumor to grow"
- Dosing: Daily oral; "take it every morning with or without food"
- Side effect anticipation: Diarrhea, rash — manageable; what to report immediately (breathing changes)
- Duration expectation: "We expect to continue as long as it's working and you're tolerating it"

### Stage 3: On-Treatment Monitoring (Ongoing)
**Standard monitoring**:
- CT scan every 8–12 weeks (per center protocol)
- LVEF assessment at baseline; repeat if symptoms of cardiac dysfunction
- ECG at baseline; follow-up if QTc borderline or QT-prolonging drugs added
- Patient-reported symptoms: diarrhea, rash, dyspnea (ILD monitoring)

**Key touchpoints for patient support**:
- AZ&Me program: copay card; nurse navigator; adherence calls
- Tagrisso patient resource guide at initiation
- Digital adherence app (AZ pilot in select cancer centers)

### Stage 4: Disease Progression and Resistance
**Resistance mechanisms at progression on Tagrisso**:
| Mechanism | Prevalence | Post-Tagrisso Option |
|---|---|---|
| C797S (on-target) | ~15% | 4th-gen TKIs (experimental); combination strategies |
| MET amplification | ~20% | Savolitinib + osimertinib (Phase 2) |
| KRAS, BRAF activation | ~10% | MEK inhibitors; histologic re-biopsy |
| Histologic transformation (SCLC) | ~5–10% | Platinum-based chemotherapy |
| Unknown / no identified resistance | ~30–40% | Platinum-based chemotherapy; WBRT if CNS progression |

**Liquid biopsy at progression**: Critical for resistance mechanism identification; avoid empiric chemotherapy if targetable resistance identified

## 3. PATIENT JOURNEY MAP — ADJUVANT NSCLC

### Stage 1: Surgical Resection
**Patient experience**: Surgery for lung cancer is one of the most psychologically intense moments — "they got it all" vs uncertainty of recurrence

**Key gap**: ~58% of eligible resected EGFR-mutant patients do NOT receive adjuvant Tagrisso (underpenetration)

**Why?**:
| Barrier | Prevalence | Root Cause |
|---|---|---|
| EGFR not tested post-resection | ~28% of eligible patients | Surgeons don't order; pathology not reflexive |
| Surgeon-to-oncologist handoff gap | ~22% of eligible patients | No established multidisciplinary pathway for adjuvant referral |
| Patient not counseled on recurrence risk | ~18% | Surgeon communication gap: "We got it all" → patient declines oncology referral |
| Oncologist adjuvant concern | ~15% | 3-year daily therapy concern; adherence uncertainty; "will it work for Stage IB?" |
| Financial/access barriers | ~10% | High drug cost; 3 years of daily therapy |

### Stage 2: Adjuvant Treatment Discussion
**ADAURA data that resonates most with patients** (research finding):
1. "Your chance of being disease-free at 5 years: 65.8% vs 26.2% if not treated" — most impactful stat
2. "Your risk of the cancer coming back in the brain is much lower: 98% vs 85% stayed brain cancer-free" — resonates with fear of CNS disease
3. "OS benefit: 88% vs 78% alive at 5 years" — survival framing most patient-centric

**Patient archetype — Adjuvant Tagrisso**:
- "The Anxious Survivor": 55–70 year old, post-resection, terrified of recurrence; wants the safest, most evidence-based option; daily pill is highly acceptable vs recurrence risk
- "The Informed Researcher": Has read ADAURA data; arrives at oncology appointment knowing they want Tagrisso; just needs medical confirmation and access support

## 5. ADHERENCE DRIVERS AND BARRIERS

### 5.1 Adherence Profile (Tagrisso vs Conventional Chemotherapy)
- Oral targeted therapy generally shows **higher adherence** than IV chemotherapy regimens
- Tagrisso 12-month adherence in adjuvant setting (RWE): ~78–82% (ADAURA RCT: ~92%)
- Adjuvant setting has unique adherence challenge: **asymptomatic patients taking daily oral therapy for 3 years** (cancer is "gone" but treatment continues)

### 5.2 Top Adherence Drivers (Tagrisso)
1. **Fear of recurrence** — most powerful adherence motivator; patients who understand the risk without Tagrisso are 2.8× more adherent
2. **Oncologist relationship** — HCP relationship quality is the #1 modifiable predictor of adherence
3. **AZ&Me program enrollment** — copay assistance; nurse navigator; shown +18% adherence improvement vs unenrolled
4. **Tolerability** — well-tolerated patients are more persistent; rash and diarrhea Grade ≥2 increase discontinuation 3.5×
5. **Simple regimen** — once-daily oral with no dietary restriction resonates for life integration

### 5.3 Top Adherence Barriers
1. **Asymptomatic disease (adjuvant)**: "I feel fine — do I really need to keep taking this pill?" — 34% of adjuvant discontinuers cite this
2. **Financial toxicity**: 3 years × ~$18,000–$25,000/year even with insurance = cumulative burden
3. **Side effect fatigue**: Ongoing paronychia, diarrhea — not severe but persistent; 22% of patients report quality-of-life impact
4. **Life disruption**: Travel concerns; refrigeration not needed (advantage), but daily schedule integration
5. **Anxiety at scheduled scans**: "Scanxiety" often prompts medication hoarding, dose skipping around scan times

### 5.4 Adherence Rates by Indication

| Indication | 12-Month Adherence (RWE) | Key Factor |
|---|---|---|
| 1L Metastatic | 85–88% | High motivation; disease visible; high-stakes |
| 2L T790M+ | 80–84% | Also high motivation; patients often heavily pre-treated |
| Adjuvant (Stage IB–IIIA) | 78–82% | Lower motivation; "invisible" recurrence risk; 3-year duration |
