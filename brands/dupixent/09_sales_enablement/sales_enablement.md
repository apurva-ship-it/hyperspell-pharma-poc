## 1. SALES CALL GUIDE — STRUCTURED CALL MODEL

### 1.1 Pre-Call Planning (5 minutes before entering office)
| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **CRM review** | Review HCP's last 3 call notes; recent NRx data; open PA issues; program invitations pending |
| **Prescribing behavior** | Check decile change, TRx trend, competitive prescribing shift (e.g., Rinvoq increase) |
| **HCP profile** | Refresh on personal details, professional interests, research involvement, KOL status |
| **Objective setting** | One clear, measurable call objective (e.g., "Secure commitment to start next severe AD pt on Dupixent") |
| **Leave-behind selection** | Choose ONE MLR-approved piece relevant to call objective (not every detail at once) |
| **MyWay updates** | Check for any PA denials, patient support issues, or reimbursement updates in doctor's panel |

### 1.2 Opening (2–3 minutes)
**Goal**: Establish rapport, anchor the call to HCP priorities, earn the next minute.

**Proven Opening Frameworks**:

**Evidence-Led Open**:
"Dr. [Name], at [Congress/JAAD/NEJM] there was a remarkable new dataset from the 5-year LIBERTY extension — patients maintained EASI-75 at the same rate as their initial response with no new safety signals. I wanted to share that today because I know your patients are asking about long-term outcomes."

**Patient-Insight Open**:
"Dr. [Name], I spoke with your medical assistant last week and she mentioned you've had some cases where AD patients are asking about oral options. I wanted to discuss how Dupixent compares — especially on the safety profile for patients who are hesitant about JAK inhibitors."

**Business-Value Open** (for Tier 1):
"Dr. [Name], I saw that the three new PA approvals for your patients came through quickly via MyWay. I wanted to touch base on whether there are any pending cases we can accelerate — and share some new COPD type 2 data that might be relevant for your pulmonology colleagues."

### 1.3 Probing / Needs Discovery (3–5 minutes)
**Goal**: Identify the HCP's specific barriers, patient population challenges, and competitive pressures before presenting solutions.

**Key Probing Questions**:
- "What are the types of moderate-to-severe AD patients you find hardest to treat right now?"
- "When you think about starting a biologic, what's the first concern that comes to mind?"
- "How are you currently thinking about the safety profile differences between Dupixent and JAK inhibitors when choosing between them?"
- "Are there patients you've identified with blood eosinophils ≥300 and COPD who you haven't yet considered for a biologic?"
- "When a PA comes back denied, how does that affect your prescribing decision for the next similar patient?"

**LISTEN for**: Competing brand loyalty signals, payer/PA frustration, safety concerns about biologics, patient population gaps (pediatric, COPD, comorbid), and misperceptions about Dupixent efficacy vs JAK inhibitors.

### 1.4 Presenting Solutions (5–8 minutes)
**Match solution to discovered need — do not present everything:**

| If HCP concern is... | Lead with... | Supporting data |
|---|---|---|
| JAK inhibitor efficacy advantage | "Long-term safety story" + 5-year durability | CHRONOS 52wk; RWE PROSE; BBW comparison |
| Pediatric hesitancy | LIBERTY AD INFANT data | 53% EASI-75; 6-month approval |
| COPD opportunity | BOREAS data + eos ≥300 patient ID | -30% exacerbation reduction; patient ID checklist |
| PA/access burden | MyWay program capabilities | 82% first-pass PA approval rate; case manager demo |
| Comorbid AD + asthma | Type 2 biology / comorbidity message | QUEST -65.8%; one biologic, two indications |
| Conjunctivitis concern | Management protocol + rate context | 10% rate; response to topical treatment; low rate in other indications |

**Presentation Best Practices**:
- Use the approved visual aid; reference the specific data point (trial name, HR, p-value)
- Maintain eye contact; limit visual aid to one key exhibit per message
- Check understanding: "Does that address your concern about long-term use?" before moving on
- Never make unapproved comparative claims not in the approved visual aid

### 1.5 Closing (2–3 minutes)
**Goal**: Secure a specific, observable commitment — not just "I'll think about it."

**Commitment Ladder**:
| Commitment Level | Example Close |
|---|---|
| Information commitment | "Would you like me to leave this 5-year safety publication? I can have Medical Affairs send the full e-print." |
| Trial commitment | "For your next moderate-to-severe AD patient who's failed two topicals — would you be willing to give Dupixent a try and let me know how it goes?" |
| Action commitment | "I have a MyWay case manager available this week to handle that pending PA. Can I make that call happen for your practice today?" |
| Program commitment | "We have a lunch and learn with Dr. [KOL] on COPD type 2 — your office manager mentioned Thursday works. Can I confirm that?" |

**Closing Language to Avoid**: "Just think about it," "Whatever you decide," "No pressure" — these lower commitment. Be specific, time-bound, and patient-centric.

## 2. TOP 5 OBJECTION RESPONSE SCRIPTS

### Objection Script 1: "Rinvoq shows higher EASI scores — my patients want the fastest results."
**Step 1 — Acknowledge**: "You're absolutely right that 16-week EASI data for upadacitinib shows higher numerical response rates. That's real, and I appreciate you bringing it up directly."

**Step 2 — Reframe**: "The question I'd invite you to consider is: what does 'fastest results' mean over 5 or 10 years of treatment? Many of your AD patients will be on a biologic long-term."

**Step 3 — Differentiate with data**: "Upadacitinib carries a Black Box Warning for serious infections, malignancy — including lymphoma — cardiovascular events, thrombosis, and mortality. For a 35-year-old woman with AD, that BBW has real implications. Dupixent has no BBW, no required TB or lab screening, and 5-year safety data with no new signals."

**Step 4 — Return to patient**: "For your patients with cardiovascular risk factors, older patients, or anyone with a prior malignancy — is that BBW something you want to navigate? Dupixent gives you equivalent long-term control without that conversation."

**Step 5 — Commitment close**: "Would you be open to using Dupixent for your next patient who might be in any of those higher-risk categories?"

---

### Objection Script 2: "Insurance doesn't cover it well — my patients can't afford $50,000/year."
**Step 1 — Acknowledge**: "That's one of the most common concerns I hear, and it's a real patient care issue."

**Step 2 — Redirect to net cost**: "I want to clarify: the $54,000 WAC number is the list price before rebates and copay assistance. In practice, most commercially insured patients pay $0–$60/month out of pocket with the Dupixent MyWay copay card."

**Step 3 — Quantify the program**: "MyWay covers up to $13,000/year in copay assistance. For uninsured patients who qualify — household income typically up to 500% of federal poverty level — the medication is provided at no cost."

**Step 4 — Practice relief**: "And for your practice — MyWay handles the PA submission from start to finish. Your team doesn't need to do the paperwork. Our first-pass PA approval rate is 82%."

**Step 5 — Offer a demo**: "Can I connect your office manager with a MyWay Access Advisor right now? They can walk through the process in 10 minutes and you'll have a working case by end of day."

---

### Objection Script 3: "I already have my asthma patients covered with Fasenra — why switch?"
**Step 1 — Acknowledge**: "Fasenra is a strong option for eosinophilic asthma — I have no argument with patients who are well controlled."

**Step 2 — Identify the gap**: "The patients I'd invite you to consider for Dupixent are those who aren't reaching full control on Fasenra, those who remain OCS-dependent despite benralizumab, or patients with a significant comorbid condition — like AD or CRSwNP."

**Step 3 — Present differentiating data**: "In OCS-dependent patients, VENTURE showed 48% complete OCS elimination with Dupixent. Also — Dupixent blocks IL-4 as well as IL-13, which affects IgE production and airway remodeling in ways that anti-IL-5 agents don't."

**Step 4 — Comorbidity value**: "And if your asthma patient also has moderate-to-severe AD — Dupixent is the only biologic that covers both simultaneously under approved labeling. That's a real practical difference."

---

### Objection Script 4: "I'm not convinced about Dupixent for COPD — the data seem early."
**Step 1 — Validate**: "It's a brand-new paradigm, and healthy skepticism is the right instinct. The science behind type 2 COPD is still emerging, and BOREAS is the trial that changed the conversation."

**Step 2 — Present BOREAS data**: "BOREAS enrolled 939 patients with blood eosinophils ≥300 on maximal inhaled therapy. The primary endpoint — moderate-to-severe exacerbation reduction — was -30% with p=0.0005. FEV1 improved +0.16L. St. George's Respiratory Questionnaire improved significantly. This is rigorous Phase 3 data."

**Step 3 — Patient identification**: "The first step isn't even committing to prescribe — it's identifying which of your current COPD patients have blood eos ≥300. I have a simple patient checklist. Would you look at 3–5 of your highest-burden COPD patients this week and see how many meet that threshold?"

---

### Objection Script 5: "My patient got conjunctivitis on Dupixent and wants to stop."
**Step 1 — Empathize**: "That's a frustrating situation, and I want to make sure we help you keep that patient on a therapy that's working for them systemically."

**Step 2 — Contextualize**: "Conjunctivitis in AD patients on Dupixent occurs in about 10% — it's the most common adverse event. The important nuance: it's typically mild-to-moderate and responds well to treatment."

**Step 3 — Management protocol**: "Standard approach: lubricating eye drops (artificial tears) as first line; if that's insufficient, topical antihistamine eye drops such as olopatadine; ophthalmology referral for persistent or worsening cases. In clinical practice, the vast majority of cases are managed without discontinuation."

**Step 4 — Contextualize severity**: "This is not conjunctivitis caused by infection — it's related to the inflammatory environment in AD. As skin control improves over time, many patients find the eye symptoms also improve."

**Step 5 — Close**: "Would it help if I left you our ophthalmology management protocol card? And can we agree to re-evaluate that patient's response at their next visit rather than stopping today?"

## 3. TERRITORY PLANNING FRAMEWORK

### 3.1 Account Prioritization Matrix

| Priority | Criteria | Action Frequency | Resource Investment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Platinum** | Tier 1 HCPs; high current volume; KOL potential; biologic-positive | Weekly contact; in-person minimum 2x/month | Speaker programs; advisory boards; early data access |

## 5. COACHING GUIDE FOR RSMs

### 5.1 Performance Observation Framework (3 Call Ride-Alongs/Month)
| Dimension | Green (Strong) | Yellow (Developing) | Red (Needs Improvement) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-call planning | Clear objective; CRM updated; leave-behind selected | Objective vague; CRM reviewed but not actioned | No clear objective; generic call |
| Opening | Personalized; earned time; HCP engaged | Scripted but not personalized | Generic; HCP disengaged |
| Probing | 3+ discovery questions; active listening | 1–2 probing questions; some listening | Monologue; no discovery |
| Scientific fluency | Data cited accurately with trial names and p-values | Data cited but lacking trial context | Vague or inaccurate data references |
| Objection handling | Acknowledges → Reframes → Data → Commitment | Handles some objections; misses commitment | Defensive or avoids objections |
| Close | Specific commitment obtained | Soft commitment | No commitment |

### 5.2 Coaching Conversation Model (SBI — Situation, Behavior, Impact)
> "In the call today with Dr. [Name] (Situation), when she raised the JAK inhibitor efficacy question, I noticed you immediately presented the safety BBW data without first acknowledging her point (Behavior). The HCP seemed to disengage — she was nodding but not engaging with your response (Impact). Let's practice the acknowledge-and-reframe step. Here's how I might have opened: 'You're right, that 16-week EASI data for upadacitinib is real — let me share what I think the full picture looks like over time.'"
