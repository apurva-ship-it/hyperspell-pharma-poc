## 1. Mechanism of Action — Deep Dive

### 1.1 The IL-23/Th17 Axis in Immune-Mediated Inflammatory Disease

Interleukin-23 (IL-23) is a master regulator of chronic inflammation, positioned at the apex of the Th17 immunological axis. IL-23 is a heterodimeric cytokine composed of two subunits:
- **p40 subunit** — shared with IL-12 (targeted by ustekinumab, which blocks both IL-12 and IL-23 via anti-p40)
- **p19 subunit** — unique to IL-23 (targeted selectively by risankizumab, guselkumab, and tildrakizumab)

### 1.2 Risankizumab: Selective IL-23p19 Inhibition

Risankizumab is a humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds with high affinity and selectivity to the p19 subunit of IL-23. It does not bind IL-12 (which shares the p40 subunit) — preserving IL-12–driven Th1 immunity. This selective inhibition is the mechanistic foundation of risankizumab's differentiated profile.

**Binding characteristics:**
- Kd (dissociation constant) = 0.026 nM — extremely high affinity
- Does not bind IL-12; no interference with Th1/type I immune responses
- Half-life: ~28 days (subcutaneous), supporting Q12W dosing

### 1.3 Downstream Consequences of IL-23 Inhibition

**IL-23 signaling cascade:**
1. IL-23 binds IL-23 receptor (IL-23R) on Th17 cells, ILC3s, and CD8+ T cells
2. JAK1/TYK2 phosphorylation → STAT3 activation
3. STAT3 drives transcription of:
   - IL-17A, IL-17F (keratinocyte-activating; neutrophil-recruiting)
   - IL-22 (epithelial barrier disruption; keratinocyte proliferation in PsO)
   - TNF, IL-6 (inflammation amplification)
4. Net effect: Epidermal hyperproliferation (PsO), synovial inflammation (PsA), intestinal mucosal inflammation (CD/UC)

**Consequences of risankizumab blockade:**
- Reduced Th17 differentiation and maintenance → reduced IL-17A/F, IL-22 production
- Reduced keratinocyte hyperproliferation → PASI improvement in psoriasis
- Reduced intestinal epithelial inflammation → endoscopic and clinical remission in IBD
- Preserved IL-12-driven Th1 immunity → intact host defense against intracellular pathogens (theoretical advantage vs. ustekinumab)
- Preserved Th1 control of mycobacteria and some fungi (vs. pan-p40 blockade)

### 1.4 Why Selective p19 over p40?

**Comparison: Risankizumab (p19) vs. Ustekinumab (p40/IL-12&23)**

| Property | Risankizumab (p19) | Ustekinumab (p40) |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| IL-23 inhibition | Yes (selective) | Yes (via p40) |
| IL-12 inhibition | No | Yes |
| Th1 immunity preservation | Yes | No (Th1 suppressed) |
| Efficacy in PsO (PASI 90) | ~75% | ~42–47% (vs. risankizumab in UltIMMa) |
| CD endoscopic remission | 31.8% (SEQUENCE) | 16.2% (SEQUENCE) |
| Theoretical safety advantage | Preserved Th1 | Broader immunosuppression |

The superior clinical efficacy of p19-selective inhibition vs. p40 inhibition in both psoriasis and Crohn's disease provides empirical validation of this mechanistic hypothesis.

## 2. Key Clinical Trials — Psoriasis

### 2.1 UltIMMa-1 and UltIMMa-2

**Publications:** Gordon KB, et al. *Lancet.* 2018;392(10148):650–661.

**Design:** Two replicate, randomized, double-blind, active- and placebo-controlled Phase III trials

**Population:**
- UltIMMa-1: N=506; UltIMMa-2: N=491
- Moderate-to-severe plaque PsO (PASI ≥12, BSA ≥10%, sPGA ≥3)
- Adult patients; 0/≥1 prior biologic use

**Treatment arms:** Risankizumab 150 mg Q12W (after 2 doses at weeks 0 and 4) vs. ustekinumab (weight-based per label) vs. placebo

**Co-primary endpoints:** PASI 90 and sPGA 0/1 at week 16

| Endpoint | Risa (UltIMMa-1) | Uste (UltIMMa-1) | Placebo | Risa (UltIMMa-2) | Uste (UltIMMa-2) | Placebo |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|---------|-----------------|-----------------|---------|
| PASI 90 (Wk 16) | 75.3%* | 42.0% | 5.1% | 74.8%* | 47.5% | 2.0% |
| sPGA 0/1 (Wk 16) | 87.8%* | 63.0% | 7.8% | 83.7%* | 61.6% | 5.1% |
| PASI 100 (Wk 16) | 35.9%* | 12.0% | 0% | 30.8%* | 8.0% | 0% |
| PASI 90 (Wk 52) | 81.5%* | 50.5% | — | 80.6%* | 52.0% | — |

*p<0.001 vs. ustekinumab AND vs. placebo

**Key insight:** At week 52, PASI 90 response deepened further with risankizumab (81.5%), while ustekinumab responses were lower (50.5%). Durability advantage with risankizumab.

### 2.2 IMMvent (vs. Adalimumab)

**Publication:** Reich K, et al. *Lancet.* 2019;394(10198):576–586.

**Design:** Randomized, double-blind, active-controlled Phase III trial

**Population:** N=605; moderate-to-severe plaque PsO; biologic-naive and biologic-experienced

**Comparator:** Adalimumab (TNF inhibitor; 80 mg SC Week 0, 40 mg Week 1, then 40 mg Q2W)

**Results at Week 16:**

| Endpoint | Risankizumab | Adalimumab | p-value |
|---------|--------------|-----------|---------|
| PASI 90 | 72.4% | 47.4% | <0.001 |
| PASI 100 | 39.9% | 22.5% | <0.001 |
| PASI 75 | 84.5% | 60.2% | <0.001 |
| sPGA 0/1 | 83.7% | 60.1% | <0.001 |

**Results at Week 44 (adalimumab partial responders re-randomized to risankizumab):**
- Patients with PASI 50–89 on adalimumab at week 16 re-randomized to risankizumab
- 66.0% of adalimumab partial responders achieved PASI 90 at week 44 on risankizumab
- Supports Skyrizi as a strong next-line option after inadequate adalimumab response

### 2.3 IMMhance (Long-Term Durability)

**Publication:** Blauvelt A, et al. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 2019;80(5):1421–1431.

**Design:** Randomized, double-blind, Phase III; withdrawal/re-treatment sub-study

**Population:** N=507; moderate-to-severe plaque PsO

**Key finding:** Among week-52 responders (sPGA 0/1) who were re-randomized to placebo withdrawal vs. continued treatment:
- Continued risankizumab: 87% maintained sPGA 0/1 at week 104
- Placebo withdrawal: 61% maintained sPGA 0/1 at week 88 (some durability off-drug)
- Re-treatment after relapse: 98.5% achieved sPGA 0/1 within 16 weeks — consistent re-treatment response

### 2.4 IMMerge (Long-Term; 5-Year Data)

- Phase III, 5-year follow-up
- 84% of patients maintained PASI 90 response at week 260 (5 years)
- Consistent safety profile; no new safety signals over 5 years
- Supports long-term disease management message

## 4. Key Clinical Trials — Crohn's Disease

### 4.1 ADVANCE (Induction — CD)

**Publication:** Ferrante M, et al. *Lancet.* 2022;399(10340):2031–2046.

**Design:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled induction trial

**Population:** N=931; moderate-to-severe CD; prior inadequate response/intolerance to conventional therapy and/or biologic therapy

**Induction regimen:** Risankizumab 600 mg IV at weeks 0, 4, 8

**Primary endpoints (week 12):** Clinical remission (CDAI <150) and endoscopic response

| Endpoint (Week 12) | Risa 600 mg IV | Placebo | p-value |
|-------------------|---------------|---------|---------|
| Clinical remission (CDAI) | 45.1% | 24.1% | <0.001 |
| Endoscopic response | 40.3% | 12.1% | <0.001 |
| Clinical response | 62.6% | 40.5% | <0.001 |

### 4.2 MOTIVATE (Induction — CD, biologic-experienced)

**Publication:** Ferrante M, et al. *Lancet.* 2022;399(10340):2031–2046. (Same publication, sister trial)

**Population:** N=618; moderate-to-severe CD; prior biologic failure

**Induction results (Week 12):**

| Endpoint | Risa 600 mg IV | Placebo | p-value |
|---------|---------------|---------|---------|
| Clinical remission | 42.2% | 19.6% | <0.001 |
| Endoscopic response | 29.4% | 11.3% | <0.001 |

### 4.3 FORTIFY (Maintenance — CD)

**Publication:** Loftus EV, et al. *Lancet.* 2023;401(10385):1334–1346.

**Design:** Randomized withdrawal maintenance trial (responders to ADVANCE/MOTIVATE re-randomized)

**Population:** N=462; ADVANCE/MOTIVATE responders; re-randomized to risankizumab 360 mg Q8W SC vs. placebo

**Primary endpoint (Week 52):** Clinical remission and endoscopic remission

| Endpoint (Week 52) | Risa 360 mg Q8W | Placebo | p-value |
|-------------------|-----------------|---------|---------|
| Clinical remission | 52.2% | 40.7% | <0.01 |
| Endoscopic remission | 46.5% | 22.2% | <0.001 |
| Endoscopic response (maint) | 61.4% | 37.9% | <0.001 |

### 4.4 SEQUENCE (Head-to-Head vs. Ustekinumab — CD)

**Publication:** D'Haens G, et al. *N Engl J Med.* 2024;390(2):114–125.

**Design:** Randomized, double-blind, active-controlled Phase IIIb head-to-head superiority trial

**Population:** N=745; moderate-to-severe CD; prior inadequate response or intolerance to ≥1 anti-TNF therapy

**Comparator:** Ustekinumab (standard induction: IV weight-based, then 90 mg SC Q8W maintenance)

**Primary endpoint:** Endoscopic remission at week 48

| Endpoint (Week 48) | Risankizumab | Ustekinumab | p-value |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| **Endoscopic remission** | **31.8%** | **16.2%** | **0.0003** |
| Clinical remission (CDAI <150) | 58.6% | 39.5% | <0.001 |
| Endoscopic response | 46.6% | 25.8% | <0.001 |
| Composite endpoint (clin remission + endoscopic response) | 46.4% | 27.4% | <0.001 |

**This is the landmark GI evidence piece for Skyrizi:** First head-to-head superiority trial in CD proving an anti-IL-23p19 agent is superior to ustekinumab on the most objective endpoint (endoscopy).

## 5. Key Clinical Trials — Ulcerative Colitis

## 6. Safety Summary Across Indications

### 6.1 Overall Safety Profile (Pooled PsO data; N>2,400)

| Adverse Event | Risankizumab (per 100 PY) | Placebo/Comparator |
|-------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Any AE | 250.3 | 270.1 (comparator) |
| Serious AE | 7.1 | 10.5 |
| Infections (any) | 62.1 | 64.8 |
| Serious infections | 1.5 | 2.3 |
| Tinea infections | 0.9 | 0.2 (slightly elevated) |
| MACE (major CV events) | 0.4 | 0.5 |
| Malignancy (excl. NMSC) | 0.4 | 0.5 |
| Deaths | 0.2 | 0.3 |

**Key safety differentiators:**
- No Black Box Warning (vs. TNF inhibitors: BBW for serious infections, malignancy; vs. JAK inhibitors: BBW for MACE, malignancy, serious infections)
- No contraindication in IBD (vs. IL-17 inhibitors: contraindicated in IBD, including in patients with concurrent PsO)
- Tinea infections: Slightly elevated rate (dose-dependent; manageable); rare serious fungal infections
