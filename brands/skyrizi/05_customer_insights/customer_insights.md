## 1. HCP Segmentation

### 1.1 Segmentation by Specialty

Skyrizi is prescribed across three distinct specialist communities, each with different needs and competitive dynamics:

| Specialty | Primary Indication | Prescribing Behavior | Key Insight |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Dermatologists | Plaque PsO, PsA (skin) | High volume; strong IL-23 familiarity; PASI-driven outcomes | PASI 90/100 aspiration is the primary value driver |
| Rheumatologists | PsA (joint), PsO skin component | Moderate Skyrizi adoption; IL-17 dominant in PsA; JAK concern increasing | ACR50+ data and concurrent skin clearance differentiating |
| Gastroenterologists | CD, UC | Newest Skyrizi market; established vedolizumab and anti-TNF use; SEQUENCE data is changing behavior | SEQUENCE superiority vs. ustekinumab is the primary conversion driver |

### 1.2 Skyrizi Prescriber Segments

#### Champions — "PASI 100 Champions" (~18% of dermatologists, ~55% of PsO volume)
- Profile: Academic dermatologists; high-volume PsO/PsA centers; early adopters; frequently speak about PASI 100 as the treatment target
- Needs: Long-term durability data (IMMerge 5-year); new indication data as launched; pipeline updates (HS, pediatric)
- Engagement: KOL advisory boards; ISSs (investigator-sponsored studies); speaker bureau; congress symposia

#### Adopters — "Evidence-Guided" (~35% of dermatologists, ~30% of volume)
- Profile: Private practice dermatologists; use Skyrizi for moderate-to-severe PsO; may switch to IL-17 for rapidly progressive or IBD-free patients
- Needs: Practical dosing guides; patient support program tools; real-world data; PASI 90 vs. PASI 100 outcome aspiration development
- Engagement: Rep visits; lunch-and-learns; case-based education; RWE publications

#### Resistors — "IL-17 Loyalists" (~20% of dermatologists, ~10% of volume)
- Profile: Prescribers who prefer IL-17 for rapid onset; comfort with established secukinumab/ixekizumab experience; skeptical of slower IL-23 onset
- Needs: Onset-of-action education (IL-23 achieves peak at 12 weeks but maintains longer); long-term PASI 90 durability at 5 years (IMMerge); IBD-safety story
- Engagement: Targeted CME on long-term outcomes vs. short-term onset; IBD comorbidity patient scenario training

#### GI Adopters — "SEQUENCE-Converted" (~22% of IBD gastroenterologists, growing rapidly post-SEQUENCE)
- Profile: Academic GI specialists; early Skyrizi CD prescribers; motivated by SEQUENCE superiority data
- Needs: Practical IV infusion guidance; post-TNF patient identification; biologic-experienced step therapy support; UC launch data (INSPIRE/COMMAND)
- Engagement: DDW/ACG congress; IBD symposia; SEQUENCE publication-driven education; rheumatology/GI cross-speciality programs

## 2. Patient Journey Mapping

### 2.1 Psoriasis Patient Journey

**Stage 1: Disease Onset**
- Age range at diagnosis: Bimodal peaks at 20–30 years and 50–60 years
- Initial symptoms: Plaques on elbows, knees, scalp; often dismissed as "dandruff" or "eczema"
- Healthcare contact: PCP typically first; misdiagnosis rate 28% for at least 6 months
- Patient mindset: Embarrassment; "Is this contagious?"; social anxiety; impact on relationships
- Brand opportunity: Disease awareness; "Ask about biologics" patient education

**Stage 2: Diagnosis**
- Event: Dermatologist diagnosis; initial topical therapy (corticosteroids, vitamin D analogues)
- Patient mindset: Relief at diagnosis; hope that topicals will control; reluctance to consider biologics ("strong drugs")
- HCP action: Initial topicals; methotrexate if extensive; phototherapy in some cases
- Brand opportunity: Education on biologic therapy for moderate-to-severe disease; patient advocacy partnerships

**Stage 3: Conventional Therapy Failure / Biologic Initiation**
- Event: PASI ≥12, BSA ≥10%, or significantly impaired quality of life (DLQI ≥10)
- Patient mindset: Frustrated with "not clear enough"; increasingly vocal about impact on work, relationships, mental health
- HCP trigger: Inadequate response to topicals/MTX; patient quality of life discussions
- Brand opportunity: "Clearer is possible — PASI 90/100 is achievable with Skyrizi"; DLQI improvement messaging

**Stage 4: Biologic Use (Often TNF or IL-17 First)**
- Patient mindset: Significant improvement; may accept PASI 75 as "good enough"; monthly injections become routine
- HCP action: Quarterly monitoring; assess ongoing response
- Brand opportunity: "Beyond PASI 75 — are you satisfied with 75% better? Could you be 90% or 100% clear?"

**Stage 5: Biologic Switch to IL-23 (Skyrizi)**
- Event: Inadequate response; side effects; desire for less frequent dosing (Q12W vs. Q4W)
- Trigger: New IBD diagnosis; IBD concern; desire for improved clearance; convenience
- HCP decision: Switch to Skyrizi based on deeper efficacy data and/or IBD safety profile
- Brand opportunity: Smooth transition program; mySkyrizi support; 4-injection-per-year convenience story

**Stage 6: Long-Term Maintenance (Ideal)**
- Patient milestones: PASI 90/100 achievement; DLQI 0/1; social re-engagement
- Key patient quote (from advisory board): "When my skin is clear, I'm a different person. I go to the beach. I date again."
- Brand opportunity: 5-year durability data (IMMerge 84% maintenance at 5 years); adherence support

### 2.2 Crohn's Disease Patient Journey

**Stage 1: Disease Onset**
- Average age at CD diagnosis: 20–30 years (young adult predominant)
- Symptoms: Abdominal pain, diarrhea, weight loss, fatigue; average diagnostic delay: 9 months
- Emotional impact: High; affects education, career, relationships, sexual function

**Stage 2: Conventional Therapy**
- Aminosalicylates (limited CD benefit), steroids (acute management), immunomodulators (azathioprine, 6-MP)
- Most patients with moderate-to-severe CD will fail conventional therapy and require biologics

**Stage 3: First Biologic (Anti-TNF)**
- Standard of care: Adalimumab (Humira/biosimilars) or infliximab as first biologic
- Primary non-response: ~30% of CD patients; secondary loss of response: ~50% by year 5
- Post-TNF failure: Second biologic needed — major inflection point

**Stage 4: Second-Line Biologic (Post-TNF)**
- Patient mindset: Experienced, informed; fear of surgery; desperate for remission
- HCP decision: Ustekinumab, vedolizumab, or increasingly Skyrizi (post-SEQUENCE)
- **SEQUENCE opportunity:** Post-TNF CD patients = ideal Skyrizi population (SEQUENCE was conducted in this population)
- Brand opportunity: SEQUENCE data; endoscopic remission target (not just symptom control)

**Stage 5: Deep Remission and Long-Term Management**
- Endoscopic remission as treatment target: Growing consensus for "treat-to-target" in CD
- Skyrizi FORTIFY: 46.5% endoscopic remission at week 52 (vs. 22.2% placebo)
- Brand opportunity: Endoscopic remission as the new benchmark; "mucosal healing" messaging

## 3. Unmet Needs Analysis

### 3.1 Psoriasis Unmet Needs

| Unmet Need | Patient Prevalence | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| PASI 75 as ceiling (not 90/100 being targeted) | 47% of biologic patients | Undertreatment; patients not achieving full benefit |
| IBD comorbidity — fear of IL-17 use | 18–25% of PsO patients | Many patients on sub-optimal therapy due to IBD concern |
| Psoriatic arthritis missed/underdiagnosed | 30% of PsO patients develop PsA | Dermatologists not screening for joint involvement |
| Convenience / injection frequency | 41% cite as top concern | Monthly injections (IL-17) vs. Q12W (Skyrizi) |
| Mental health impact of PsO not addressed | 62% report significant anxiety/depression | QoL tools (DLQI) should be routine |
| Pediatric PsO — no approved biologic options | Children 12–17 years | Phase III Skyrizi pediatric trial ongoing |

### 3.2 Crohn's Disease Unmet Needs

| Unmet Need | Patient Prevalence | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------------|
| Post-TNF failure — limited evidence-based options | ~60% of moderate-severe CD | SEQUENCE directly addresses this group |
| Endoscopic remission rarely achieved | Only 20–30% with any biologic | Deep remission associated with surgery avoidance |
| Perianal disease not fully addressed by current agents | 25–30% of CD patients | Ongoing unmet need; targeted sub-analyses needed |
| Patient education on mucosal healing concept | 68% unaware | Opportunity for "treat-to-target" patient engagement |
| Fear of IV infusion (CD induction) | 34% cite as barrier | In-office infusion support, home health options needed |

## 5. Prescriber Behavior Insights

### 5.1 Key Prescribing Drivers — Dermatologists (Survey N=348, Q3 2023)

**Top reasons for choosing Skyrizi in PsO:**
1. "PASI 90/100 efficacy data vs. ustekinumab" — 74%
2. "Q12W dosing convenience for patients" — 68%
3. "Strong durability at 5 years" — 61%
4. "No IBD contraindication — safe for patients with GI comorbidities" — 57%
5. "Head-to-head data vs. adalimumab (IMMvent)" — 43%

**Top reasons for NOT choosing Skyrizi:**
1. "Slower onset vs. IL-17 — patients want fast results" — 52%
2. "Insurance issues / step therapy requiring prior IL-17 or MTX failure" — 48%
3. "Guselkumab has comparable efficacy at same mechanism" — 31%
4. "No structural data for PsA joint damage" — 28%
5. "Less experience than secukinumab" — 22%

### 5.2 Key Prescribing Drivers — Gastroenterologists (Survey N=224, Q1 2024)

**Top reasons for choosing Skyrizi in CD:**
1. "SEQUENCE head-to-head superiority vs. ustekinumab (endoscopic remission)" — 81%
2. "Treat-to-target endoscopic remission goal" — 67%
3. "No BBW vs. JAK inhibitors" — 62%
4. "IV induction then SC maintenance — convenient transition" — 55%
5. "AbbVie support and access infrastructure" — 44%

**Top barriers for GI adoption:**
1. "Habitual use of vedolizumab or ustekinumab; comfort with established agents" — 59%
2. "IV induction requires infusion center logistics" — 47%
3. "Biosimilar ustekinumab is much cheaper" — 54% (major barrier)
4. "Unsure of place in therapy vs. vedolizumab or JAK inhibitors" — 38%

### 5.3 Patient Self-Advocacy Trend (PsO)

- 44% of new Skyrizi starts in 2023 involved patient-initiated discussion about biologic therapy with dermatologist
- "Nothing is Everything" DTC campaign drove 2.8M unique website visits; 18% requested HCP conversation guide
- Social media PsO community (Facebook, Instagram, TikTok) influencing biologic choices; patient influencer partnerships important for brand engagement

### 6.2 IBD Population Demographics

- CD peak incidence at 20–30 years; high impact on career and family formation
- Higher prevalence in Ashkenazi Jewish populations; genetic component
- Increasing incidence in non-White populations (formerly considered lower-risk)
- AbbVie equity in access initiative: Subsidized infusion center support for Medicaid CD patients
