## 1. Core Brand Claim (Master Claim)

**Approved Master Claim:**
> "Eliquis (apixaban) is the only oral anticoagulant proven superior to warfarin in reducing stroke risk in NVAF while also demonstrating significant reductions in major bleeding and all-cause mortality — in a single landmark trial."

**Data Anchor:** ARISTOTLE; N=18,201; Stroke HR 0.79 (p=0.01); Major bleed HR 0.69 (p<0.001); Mortality HR 0.89 (p=0.047)
**Approved Use:** DTP promotional materials, sales aid, HCP digital platforms
**Restriction:** Requires full ISI in all materials; "only oral anticoagulant" claim requires careful MLR review — approved based on the totality of the ARISTOTLE triple endpoint finding

## 2. MLR-Approved Key Messages (HCP-Directed)

### Key Message 1 — Superior Stroke Prevention

**Short form (headline):**
> "Eliquis demonstrated superior reduction in stroke and systemic embolism compared to warfarin in ARISTOTLE — the only DOAC to achieve efficacy superiority."

**Long form (body copy):**
> In the ARISTOTLE trial, a randomized, double-blind trial of 18,201 patients with nonvalvular atrial fibrillation, apixaban (Eliquis) reduced the risk of stroke or systemic embolism by 21% compared to well-managed warfarin (HR 0.79; 95% CI 0.66–0.95; p=0.01). Eliquis was the only direct oral anticoagulant to demonstrate statistically significant superiority to warfarin on the primary efficacy endpoint of stroke and systemic embolism.

**Approved supporting visuals:** ARISTOTLE Kaplan-Meier stroke curve; efficacy comparison table (vs. other DOACs from respective trials)
**Note:** "Only DOAC" to show superiority is a context-sensitive claim — RE-LY dabigatran 150mg also showed superiority but different trial design; MLR guidance: use "in ARISTOTLE vs. warfarin" framing; approved as written above

---

### Key Message 2 — Bleeding Safety Superiority

**Short form:**
> "Eliquis reduced major bleeding by 31% compared to warfarin in ARISTOTLE, including a 58% reduction in intracranial hemorrhage."

**Long form:**
> In ARISTOTLE, apixaban demonstrated statistically significant reductions in major bleeding compared to warfarin:
> - Major bleeding: 2.13% vs. 3.09% per year (HR 0.69; 95% CI 0.60–0.80; p<0.001) — a 31% relative reduction
> - Intracranial hemorrhage (ICH): 0.33% vs. 0.80% per year (HR 0.42; 95% CI 0.30–0.58; p<0.001) — a 58% relative reduction
> - Fatal bleeding: 0.19% vs. 0.38% per year (HR 0.50; 95% CI 0.33–0.75; p<0.001)
>
> These bleeding reductions were achieved simultaneously with superior stroke prevention — a clinical combination achieved by no other oral anticoagulant vs. warfarin in NVAF.

**Approved supporting visuals:** ARISTOTLE bleeding outcomes forest plot; ICH comparison bar chart

---

### Key Message 3 — All-Cause Mortality Benefit

**Short form:**
> "Eliquis is the only oral Factor Xa inhibitor to demonstrate a significant reduction in all-cause mortality compared to warfarin in NVAF."

**Long form:**
> In ARISTOTLE, apixaban significantly reduced all-cause mortality by 11% compared to warfarin (HR 0.89; 95% CI 0.80–0.99; p=0.047). This represents an absolute reduction of 0.42 deaths per 100 patient-years. No other approved Factor Xa inhibitor has demonstrated a statistically significant reduction in all-cause mortality versus warfarin in a prospective NVAF trial.

**Note:** Dabigatran 150mg in RE-LY also showed mortality benefit (HR 0.88, nominally significant); this messaging is approved with "Factor Xa inhibitor" qualifier.

---

### Key Message 4 — Superior Major Bleeding in VTE (AMPLIFY)

**Short form:**
> "Eliquis demonstrated 69% lower major bleeding vs. standard anticoagulant therapy in acute VTE treatment — with non-inferior efficacy."

**Long form:**
> In the AMPLIFY trial (N=5,395), apixaban demonstrated:
> - Non-inferior efficacy: 2.3% recurrent VTE vs. 2.7% with conventional therapy (RR 0.84; 95% CI 0.60–1.18; non-inferiority p<0.001)
> - Major bleeding: 0.6% vs. 1.8% (RR 0.31; 95% CI 0.17–0.55; p<0.001) — 69% reduction
> - Combined major + CRNM bleeding: 4.3% vs. 9.7% (RR 0.44; p<0.001)
>
> Apixaban is the only oral anticoagulant with an oral-only dosing regimen for acute VTE (no parenteral lead-in required in most patients).

---

### Key Message 5 — Extended VTE Prevention (AMPLIFY-EXT)

**Short form:**
> "Eliquis 2.5 mg BID reduced recurrent VTE by 67% vs. placebo during extended anticoagulation — with a bleeding rate comparable to placebo."

**Long form:**
> In AMPLIFY-EXT, patients who completed initial anticoagulation were randomized to apixaban 2.5mg BID, 5mg BID, or placebo for up to 12 additional months. Apixaban 2.5mg BID:
> - Reduced recurrent VTE or VTE-related death by 67% vs. placebo (3.8% vs. 11.6%; RR 0.33; 95% CI 0.19–0.56; p<0.001)
> - Major bleeding rate: 0.2% — not significantly different from placebo (0.5%)
>
> The 2.5mg BID dose is specifically approved and optimized for extended secondary VTE prevention after completion of 6 months of therapeutic anticoagulation.

---

### Key Message 6 — Dosing Simplicity (No Food, No Monitoring)

**Short form:**
> "Eliquis requires no routine INR monitoring, no dietary restrictions, and provides predictable anticoagulation — simplifying chronic management vs. warfarin."

**Long form:**
> Unlike warfarin, Eliquis does not require routine coagulation monitoring (no INR testing), has no significant dietary interactions (unlike warfarin's vitamin K dependence), and has predictable pharmacokinetics. This simplification can improve the quality of anticoagulation therapy for patients who found warfarin management burdensome. In real-world registries, patients switching from warfarin to Eliquis report significantly higher satisfaction and lower treatment-related burden.

---

### Key Message 7 — Established Safety Record and Reversal

**Short form:**
> "Eliquis has over a decade of post-approval safety experience, and a dedicated reversal agent — Andexxa — is available for life-threatening or uncontrolled bleeding."

**Long form:**
> Since FDA approval in 2012, Eliquis has accumulated over 13 years of real-world safety data across tens of millions of patients globally. Real-world evidence consistently confirms the ARISTOTLE safety profile. For rare instances of life-threatening or uncontrolled bleeding, Andexxa (andexanet alfa) is FDA-approved to reverse the anticoagulant effect of apixaban. In the ANNEXA-4 study, Andexxa achieved hemostatic efficacy in 82% of patients with acute major bleeding.

## 4. Objection Handlers (MLR-Reviewed)

### Objection 1: "Xarelto is easier — once daily."

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"Once-daily dosing is a real practical benefit. Here's the clinical trade-off: In ROCKET-AF, rivaroxaban was non-inferior on stroke prevention and showed no significant bleeding reduction vs. warfarin. In ARISTOTLE, Eliquis was superior on stroke AND reduced major bleeding by 31% AND cut intracranial hemorrhage by 58%. For your atrial fibrillation patient — especially if elderly, with a history of falls, or prior bleed — the dramatic ICH reduction with Eliquis is clinically critical. A brain bleed on anticoagulation carries 50% 30-day mortality. Twice-daily is manageable with a phone reminder; an intracranial hemorrhage is not manageable."

---

### Objection 2: "All DOACs are the same — I'll use whatever's on formulary."

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"Formulary status matters — and Eliquis is preferred on the majority of commercial plans and Medicare Part D. But within the DOAC class, meaningful clinical differences exist in the data. The ARISTOTLE trial is the only DOAC trial vs. warfarin to show: (1) stroke superiority, (2) major bleed reduction, and (3) all-cause mortality reduction — all in the same trial. These three combined are unique to Eliquis and are especially relevant for your highest-risk patients."

---

### Objection 3: "I'm worried about bleeding — the patient takes NSAIDs regularly."

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"NSAID co-use does increase GI bleeding risk with any anticoagulant. The key decision framework is: what is the patient's stroke risk on Eliquis vs. bleeding risk? For a patient with CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2, untreated AF carries a 3–5% annual stroke risk — often devastating. We counsel on minimizing NSAID use; switching to acetaminophen where possible. If NSAID use is unavoidable, Eliquis still has the lowest major bleed profile among the DOACs vs. warfarin. The AHA/ACC guidelines continue to recommend anticoagulation in this population despite NSAID co-use."

---

### Objection 4: "The patient is elderly and at risk for falls."

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"Fear of falls is one of the most common reasons physicians don't start anticoagulation in elderly AF patients — but the data doesn't support withholding therapy. Studies show a patient would need to fall more than 295 times a year for the fall-related bleed risk to outweigh the stroke prevention benefit of anticoagulation. More importantly, if a fall does occur, Eliquis's 58% lower ICH rate vs. warfarin means the risk of intracranial injury is significantly lower than with VKA therapy. For elderly AF patients, Eliquis — particularly at the 2.5mg dose if dose-reduction criteria apply — is the safest anticoagulant option."

---

### Objection 5: "My patient's kidneys aren't great — I'm nervous."

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"Eliquis is actually the most renally safe DOAC. Only 27% of apixaban is renally eliminated — compare that to 80% for dabigatran. For patients with eGFR 25–50, no dose adjustment is required based on renal function alone. The three-criterion dose reduction rule (age ≥80, weight ≤60 kg, creatinine ≥1.5) captures the patients who need 2.5mg BID. ARISTOTLE included patients with elevated creatinine, and the benefit-risk was consistent in that subgroup. CKD patients actually benefit enormously from stroke prevention since they're at dramatically elevated AF risk."

---

### Objection 6: "What if there's a serious bleed? How do you reverse it?"

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"This is an important and valid concern. Andexxa (andexanet alfa) is FDA-approved specifically to reverse apixaban and rivaroxaban in cases of life-threatening or uncontrolled bleeding. In the ANNEXA-4 study, Andexxa achieved hemostatic efficacy in 82% of patients — comparable to FFP reversal for warfarin. Andexxa is available at most Level I and II trauma centers and many community hospitals. Additionally, non-specific hemostatic agents (PCC/4-factor PCC) can be used in centers without Andexxa access. The half-life of apixaban is 8–15 hours — a relatively short window."

---

### Objection 7: "The patient was stable on warfarin — why switch?"

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"Warfarin works — but the evidence consistently shows better outcomes with Eliquis. Even in ARISTOTLE, where warfarin TTR was 66% (better than most real-world settings where TTR averages 55%), Eliquis still demonstrated stroke superiority and bleeding reduction. In real-world settings with TTR often 50–60%, the advantage of Eliquis is even larger. The AHA/ACC 2023 AF guidelines recommend DOACs over warfarin for all eligible NVAF patients. For a stable patient, the conversation is about risk reduction — even well-managed warfarin carries ICH risk 2× higher than Eliquis."
