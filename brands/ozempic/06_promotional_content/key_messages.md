## 1. Core Brand Claim (Master Claim)

**Approved Master Claim:**
> "Ozempic (semaglutide) injection is the only once-weekly GLP-1 receptor agonist proven to reduce the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events — cardiovascular death, non-fatal myocardial infarction, and non-fatal stroke — in adults with type 2 diabetes mellitus and established cardiovascular disease."

**Data Anchor:** SUSTAIN-6; HR 0.74 (95% CI 0.58–0.95); p=0.016; N=3,297
**Approved Use:** DTP (direct to professional) promotional materials, sales aid, digital HCP platforms
**Restriction:** Cannot be used in DTC without full ISI reference

## 2. MLR-Approved Key Messages (HCP-Directed)

### Key Message 1 — Cardiovascular Outcomes

**Short form (headline):**
> "Ozempic demonstrated a statistically significant 26% reduction in the risk of MACE in adults with T2D and established CVD."

**Long form (body copy):**
> In the SUSTAIN-6 cardiovascular outcomes trial, Ozempic significantly reduced the risk of the 3-point MACE composite endpoint (cardiovascular death, non-fatal myocardial infarction, and non-fatal stroke) by 26% compared to placebo (HR 0.74; 95% CI 0.58–0.95; p=0.016) in 3,297 adults with type 2 diabetes mellitus and established cardiovascular disease or high CV risk. The median follow-up was 2.1 years.

**Approved supporting visuals:** SUSTAIN-6 Kaplan-Meier curve; HR forest plot
**Not approved:** Claims extrapolating to patients without established CVD

---

### Key Message 2 — Superior Glycemic Efficacy

**Short form:**
> "Ozempic 1 mg provided superior HbA1c reduction vs. dulaglutide 1.5 mg — the highest approved dose of Trulicity — in the head-to-head SUSTAIN-7 trial."

**Long form:**
> In SUSTAIN-7, a 40-week randomized, open-label head-to-head trial (N=1,201), Ozempic 1 mg reduced HbA1c by 1.8 percentage points vs. 1.4 percentage points with dulaglutide 1.5 mg (p<0.001). Additionally, 79% of Ozempic 1 mg patients achieved the target of HbA1c <7.0% vs. 64% with dulaglutide 1.5 mg.

**Approved supporting visuals:** SUSTAIN-7 HbA1c reduction bar chart; responder analysis

---

### Key Message 3 — Clinically Meaningful Weight Reduction

**Short form:**
> "Ozempic 1 mg provided nearly twice the weight loss of dulaglutide 1.5 mg in T2D patients — a clinically meaningful difference."

**Long form:**
> In SUSTAIN-7, Ozempic 1 mg reduced body weight by 6.5 kg vs. 3.0 kg with dulaglutide 1.5 mg over 40 weeks (p<0.001). This represents a 3.5 kg absolute difference in favor of Ozempic — clinically meaningful in a population where weight management is central to cardiometabolic risk reduction.

**Approved supporting visuals:** Weight change bar chart, SUSTAIN-7

---

### Key Message 4 — Renal Protective Effects

**Short form:**
> "In the FLOW trial, semaglutide 1 mg reduced the risk of a key composite kidney endpoint by 24% in patients with T2D and CKD."

**Long form:**
> The FLOW trial (N=3,533) evaluated semaglutide 1 mg vs. placebo in patients with T2D and chronic kidney disease (eGFR 25–75 mL/min/1.73m²) over a median follow-up of 3.4 years. Semaglutide significantly reduced the composite of kidney failure, ≥50% sustained eGFR decline, cardiovascular death, and kidney disease death by 24% (HR 0.76; 95% CI 0.66–0.88; p<0.001). The trial was stopped early due to evidence of efficacy.

**Note:** FLOW data is for scientific/medical use. Ozempic CKD label pending — this claim is not yet on-label for Ozempic promotional materials. Use only in MSL/medical context until label update.

### Key Message 7 — Guideline-Recommended Therapy

**Short form:**
> "The 2025 ADA Standards of Care recommend GLP-1 receptor agonists as preferred add-on therapy in T2D patients with established ASCVD, high CV risk, or CKD — independent of HbA1c level."

**Long form:**
> According to the American Diabetes Association 2025 Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes, GLP-1 receptor agonists with proven CV benefit (including semaglutide) are recommended as preferred add-on therapy in patients with T2D and:
> - Established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) or ASCVD indicators
> - High CV risk (age ≥55 with coronary, carotid, or lower-extremity artery stenosis >50%)
> - Chronic kidney disease (eGFR 25–60 or albuminuria)
> - Heart failure
> This recommendation applies regardless of the patient's current HbA1c level. [Evidence Level: A]

## 4. Objection Handlers (MLR-Reviewed)

### Objection 1: "I'm concerned about the nausea — my patients don't tolerate it."

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"Nausea is the most common GI side effect with Ozempic, occurring in about 20% of patients at the 1mg dose — and importantly, it is usually transient. The recommended 4-week dose escalation from 0.25mg (tolerability dose) to 0.5mg significantly reduces GI burden. In clinical trials, >90% of nausea was mild to moderate, and less than 5% of patients discontinued due to GI events. Patient education about when to take the injection, eating smaller portions, and avoiding high-fat meals can significantly improve tolerance. Our OzempicCare team has dedicated nurses who coach patients through the first 12 weeks."

---

### Objection 2: "Tirzepatide shows better weight loss — why would I choose Ozempic?"

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"Tirzepatide's weight loss data is impressive. The key question for your T2D patients with cardiovascular disease is: which agent has demonstrated reduction in heart attacks and cardiovascular death? Ozempic has 7+ years of outcomes data from SUSTAIN-6 — a 26% reduction in MACE. Tirzepatide's CV outcomes trial, SURPASS-CVOT, is still ongoing. For patients where CV risk is the primary concern, Ozempic provides proven protection. And for patients where both weight and CV outcomes matter, the totality of the Ozempic evidence — including FLOW for kidney protection — provides a compelling case."

---

### Objection 3: "My patient can't afford it — the cost is too high."

**MLR-Approved Response:**

For uninsured patients or those in the coverage gap, the Ozempic Savings Offer provides the drug for $99/month with no eligibility restrictions. ---

### Objection 4: "Why should I use a GLP-1 RA instead of an SGLT2 inhibitor?"

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"SGLT2 inhibitors are excellent agents — particularly for heart failure and CKD. The ADA guidelines recommend GLP-1 RAs and SGLT2is for distinct but often complementary reasons. For patients with established ASCVD or high CV risk, GLP-1 RAs like Ozempic have stronger MACE reduction data. For patients with heart failure with reduced EF, SGLT2is have a stronger signal. Many of your complex T2D patients actually benefit from both — there's no pharmacodynamic interaction, the mechanisms are complementary, and combined use has been shown to be additive in real-world studies. Think GLP-1 RA for CV mortality and weight; SGLT2i for heart failure and CKD protection."

---

### Objection 5: "I'm worried about the thyroid cancer warning."

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"The Black Box Warning is based on rodent data at supra-clinical exposures — there is no confirmed signal of medullary thyroid carcinoma in humans. In over 100,000 patient-years of clinical trial data and more than 7 years of post-market surveillance with millions of patients, no increased incidence of MTC has been identified. The contraindication is clear and appropriate: do not use in patients with personal or family history of MTC or MEN 2. For the appropriate patient — T2D with cardiovascular risk, no MTC/MEN2 history — the benefit-risk profile is strongly favorable."

---

### Objection 6: "My patient uses insulin — can they add Ozempic?"

**MLR-Approved Response:**
"Ozempic can be used with basal insulin — in fact, this is a common and effective combination. The key safety consideration is hypoglycemia risk when combining with insulin. ADA and AACE guidelines recommend a preemptive 10–20% reduction in basal insulin dose when initiating a GLP-1 RA, with subsequent titration based on fasting glucose monitoring. In clinical practice, the HbA1c benefit is additive, and many patients are able to reduce their insulin dose over time. Prandial insulin should not be initiated at the same time as Ozempic — but existing prandial insulin can be continued with monitoring."
